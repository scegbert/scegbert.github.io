Experience

My PhD research currently focuses on three main aspects of optical measurements:

  • Building portable optical sensors (MIR dual comb spectrometers)
  • Taking those sensors to exciting places (scramjet test engines in Ohio)
  • Improving reference databases (high-temperature H2O absorption)

Building portable optical sensors

This is a prototype mobile mid-infrared dual comb. The enclosure is 19" rack-mountable and will hold all electrical and fiber components (oscillators, pump diodes, amplifiers, etc.) for each frequency comb laser. It is patterned after existing near-infrared dual comb spectrometers and generates from 1-2 μm.

I designed the free-space optics on top of the enclosure to use intra-pulse Difference Frequency Generation (ip-DFG) to convert the near-infrared light from each frequency comb to mid-infrared light spanning 3-5 μm. The beams are then combined and coupled them into mid-infrared fibers. Optical locks are also on the free-space plate.

We are excited to have a laser spanning 3-5 μm due to the strong absorption by hydrocarbon fuels, combustion products, pollutants, and other chemical species. This is an example spectrum we measured of some hydrocarbons using a GHz repetition rate laser.

Taking those sensors to exciting places

The Air Force Research Lab has funded the development of this mid-infrared laser to perform diagnostics on ramjet and other hypersonic engines. By comparing our measurement to absorption databases, we can calculate the temperature, pressure, and concentration of various gas species. Velocity is calculated from the Doppler shift by angling the laser beams, as shown above. Using these optically measured properties, we can calculate a total mass flux and species fluxes for the engine.

Improving reference databases

Accurate databases are essential for calculating thermodynamic properties using spectral measurements. I am also making a combustion-optimized spectral database of H2O absorption from 1.3-1.5 μm. This is one of the 58 measurements I took at various conditions to validate 13 unique spectral parameters for over 6000 unique absorption transitions. I developed a python wrapper and GUI to interface with a JPL-developed Fortran algorithm to expedite data processing.

This video highlights the power of dual comb spectroscopy to precisely and simultaneously measure broad spectra. An individual frequency comb "tooth" is shown in the context of a broadband spectral measurement. This is the same 1300 K measurement shown above. Dual comb spectroscopy perfectly bridges the gap between low temperature (under 400 K), broadband (over 1000 cm-1) FTIR measurements and high-temperature (over 1000 K), broadband (under 5 cm-1) tunable diode laser measurements, making it the ideal spectrometer for broadband, high-temperature database development.
