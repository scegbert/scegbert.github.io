Intro

Welcome to my personal website! I am a Ph.D. student in Greg Rieker's Precision Laser Diagnostics Lab at the University of Colorado Boulder. My specialization includes experimental optical diagnostics, optomechanical engineering, and scientific computing. I am passionate about exploring ways to ruggedize groundbreaking optical technology so that it can move out of the optics lab and into the environments where it is most needed.

This website is a platform for me to share cool pictures and videos that highlight my experiences, provide some links to connect with like-minded people, and (mostly) host my CV online. As a soon-to-graduate Ph.D. student, I am excited to continue my career in the field of optomechanical engineering. Thank you for visiting my website, and I look forward to connecting with you!

Research Experience

My research focuses on optical measurements, specifically in three main areas:

  • Designing and building a Portable Mid-Infrared Dual Comb Spectrometer
  • Performing Diagnostics on Ramjet and Other Hypersonic Engines
  • Developing a Combustion-Optimized Spectral Database of H2O Absorption

Designing and building a Portable Mid-Infrared Dual Comb Spectrometer

This is a prototype mobile mid-infrared dual comb. The enclosure is 19" rack-mountable and will hold all electrical and fiber components (oscillators, pump diodes, amplifiers, etc.) for each frequency comb laser. It is patterned after existing near-infrared dual comb spectrometers and generates light from 1-2 μm.

To convert the near-infrared light from each frequency comb to mid-infrared light spanning 3-5 μm, we use intra-pulse Difference Frequency Generation (ip-DFG), a technique developed by the Diddams group at CU. I designed a robust and compact version of the free-space optics (currently floating on top of the enclosure). These optics will eventually be protected in their own enclosure. The generated beams are then combined and coupled into mid-infrared fibers that can be routed to the measurement.

Having a spectroscopy source simultaneously spanning 3-5 μm can measure the strong absorption of hydrocarbon fuels, combustion products, pollutants, and other chemical species in that region. To illustrate this, we obtained an example spectrum of some hydrocarbons using a GHz repetition rate laser, which is shown in the image above (filtered to highlight the absorption near 3 μm).

Developing a Combustion-Optimized Spectral Database of H2O Absorption

Accurate spectral databases are crucial for calculating thermodynamic properties from spectral measurements. To this end, I am developing a combustion-optimized spectral database of H2O absorption from 1.3-1.5 μm. I collected 58 measurements under various conditions to validate 13 unique spectral parameters for over 6,000 absorption transitions. To expedite data processing, I developed a python wrapper and GUI to interface with a JPL-developed Fortran algorithm.

This video showcases the potential of dual comb spectroscopy to precisely and simultaneously measure broad spectra, starting with an individual frequency comb "tooth" before expanding to show the entire measurement. The data shown is from the same 1300 K measurement shown above.

Dual comb spectroscopy is an ideal spectrometer for broadband, high-temperature database development, as it perfectly bridges the gap between low temperature (under 400 K), broadband (over 1000 cm-1) FTIR measurements and high-temperature (over 1000 K), broadband (under 5 cm-1) tunable diode laser measurements.
